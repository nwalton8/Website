The Art of Patient Communication in Modern Nursing

How empathy and clear communication transform patient outcomes

Posted by Noah Walton on June 10, 2025

In nursing school, we spend thousands of hours mastering the clinical side of care: pharmacology, pathophysiology, assessment techniques, and procedural skills. But the skill that I've come to believe matters most — the one that can mean the difference between a patient who feels truly cared for and one who doesn't — is communication.

During my medical-surgical rotation at Utah Valley Regional Medical Center, I watched an experienced RN spend just four minutes at a patient's bedside before a procedure. She didn't go over a checklist. She asked about the patient's family, noticed the worry in his eyes, and addressed it directly. By the time she left, the patient was calm. That's not magic — that's skill.

Why Communication Is a Clinical Skill

Research consistently shows that therapeutic communication is linked to improved patient adherence, reduced anxiety, shorter hospital stays, and higher satisfaction scores. The Joint Commission has repeatedly identified communication failures as a leading root cause of serious medical errors. In other words, how we talk to our patients is not a "soft skill" — it is a patient safety issue.

The challenge is that nursing programs often treat communication as secondary to procedural training. We drill IV insertions until they become muscle memory, but we rarely rehearse how to break difficult news or how to validate a patient who's scared and resistant. Those conversations deserve the same deliberate practice.

"Patients don't care how much you know until they know how much you care." It sounds cliché, but in clinical practice, it rings absolutely true.

Three Communication Habits That Changed My Practice

Over the past year of rotations, I've tried to build three habits that have transformed how I connect with patients. First, I try to sit down whenever possible. Standing at the bedside signals that I'm in a hurry. Pulling up a chair — even briefly — communicates that I'm present. Second, I always ask open-ended questions before closed ones: "How are you feeling today?" before "Is your pain a 7 out of 10?" Third, I narrate what I'm doing. "I'm going to gently press here — let me know if anything feels uncomfortable." It sounds simple, but transparency dramatically reduces patient anxiety.

None of these techniques require extra time. They require intentionality. And that's really what patient-centered communication is: a deliberate choice to treat the person in front of you as a whole human being, not just a diagnosis on a chart.

As I move toward graduation and my RN licensure, I'm carrying this conviction with me: clinical excellence and compassionate communication aren't two separate things. The best nurses I've observed do both, simultaneously, without thinking about which one they're doing. That's the standard I'm aiming for.
